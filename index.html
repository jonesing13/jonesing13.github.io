Minneapolis Skyway Navigation Mobile App

So you can navigate the Minneapolis Skyway like a pro.

The Minneapolis Skyway is a series of enclosed footbridges that connect buildings in Downtown Minneapolis to one another.

Figure 1: Skyway footbridges along a Downtown Minneapolis street.

With these footbridges, pedestrians are able to stay connected to local events, businesses, and communities year-round (even during harsh weather).

Figure 2: Skyway footbridges connecting the YMCA building.

But the Skyway is really intimidating to "get onboarded" onto. I've been living in Downtown Minneapolis for three years and it took me (checks watch) three years to get comfortable with my little corner of the Skyway.

Figure 3: Skyway footbridges connecting the Central Downtown Library.

You see, even though the Skyway connects buildings on a city grid, it's not straight-forward like the street grids themselves. In fact, most of the footbridges connect buildings on the second floor and utilize building's hallways, atriums, or tunnels. When I am using the Skyway to get to the Walgreen's Pharmacy, I have to use the footbridge crossing 2nd St to go East, and later cross 2nd St again to go West, even though I am really just trying to go South!

Figures 4, 5, and 6: Internal examples of the Skyway.

So I got to thinking: what if there was a Skyway mobile app that I could use to navigate to locations along the Skyway, preview my route and get step-by-step instructions, see businesses details, or avoid routes that aren't available due to the hour I'm trying to use the Skyway?

Competitive Audit

I evaluated existing Skyway navigation tools, as well as other popular mobile app navigation tools to determine what would help in a Skyway navigation mobile app, and what could be omitted.

Noticeable takeaways from current tools:

  • There are no existing tools that provide navigation based on your current location.
  • While Skyway.run does provide route details, it is easier to navigate on a desktop and not a mobile device.
  • While SkywayAccess.com lets you search by name for POI, it does not identify where that building lives on a map, and you cannot "explore" the map to find business or locations in the Skyway.
  • Physical maps provide access to all forms of transportation and conneciton, but can be difficult to come by if you haven't planned in advance (when hotels or visitor centers are open).

And I still need to solve how to navigate folks to the right floor/level, which is not something that a mobile device can determine today.

Sketching a Skyway Mobile App

My main goals for this app were to empower users to:

  1. Navigate the Skyway, and
  2. Engage with local businesses and community


To get started, I sketched a few options for a Skyway mobile app, mainly drawing inspiration from the Mall of America's directory tool and Google Maps.

Figures 8 and 9: Sketches of a potential mobile app. Figure 8 (left) inspired by Google Maps. Figure 9 (right) inspired by Mall of America's directory tool.

In the end, it was six of one, half dozen of the other whether I tested an app that had a Mall-of-America-inspired landing page, with categories and lists you can browse (and showcasing a "deals/events" bottom sheet to allow partnership opportunities with local business) or a Google Maps-inspired landing page, which just plopped users right on the map.

With the intention of getting user feedback, I decided to go forward with the Mall-of-America-inspired landing page so that I could showcase what I hoped to be a "community"-minded element ("deals/events").

Research Planning

I drafted a Research Plan, but wanted to be sure to get expert feedback on my Research Script so that I felt confident that I could provide consistent experiences during user interviews. So I reached out to Brittany Schiesel, a former colleague of mine. She is currently a Director of UX Research at Blink UX.

Brittany was gracious enough to do a few rounds of review of my research plan and highlighted pretty basic, but impactful feedback for my project, including:

  1. Identify (and document) my Research Objective(s) and Key Question(s). Use these to identify the questions and tasks you need in your research, and during analysis (to draw conclusions and make recommendations).
  2. Interview 4-5 people in each "audience" you are interested in. So since I wanted to hear from folks that were unfamiliar and familiar with the Skyway, I should interview a minimum of 8 people, 4 from each group. Ideally 5 from each group.
  3. Ask open-ended questions at the end of tasks, or the interview, so that user's can share any feedback you weren't probing for.

Building A Prototype

Once I finalized my research plan, and specifically the script, it identified exactly what I needed to mock-up and build into a clickable prototype based on the tasks outlined in the script.

I used Figma to make my prototypes, and to keep the work and interactions from being "too involved" or having a lot of duplicates or versions of the same pages, I created two separate prototypes–one for each research task:

  1. Browsing the app
  2. Route/navigation to Walgreen's Pharmacy

But I was getting stuck on the navigation portion of the mock-ups and prototype for a few reasons:

  1. I was so intent on following Google Maps's "standard" of experience, that once I got to the actual steps/instructions I was determined to illustrate each step

    2. Figure 11. Each of the 25 steps needed in the route/navigation.

  2. I wasn't sure how best to display the directions:
    • Written directions at the top of the page?
    • "Hints" at next steps? (And if I did this, what kind of iconography would make sense, since these are hallways and atriums inside, and not necessarily clear "left turns" and "right turns"?
    • A blue arrow along their "route"?
  3. The steps to get from The Crossings (an apartment on the Skywyay) to Walgreen's (a pharmacy on the Skyway) were at least 25 steps, and illustrating all of that would take more time than I had before my first user interview

    4. Figure 12. Each of the 25 steps needed in the route/navigation.


In order to get my prototype working in the time I had left, I needed a quicker solution than illustrating 25 steps of the route (and 25 different interiors) "well-enough" that participants would recognize the intention of the experience for the research.

With time running down to my first interview, I really only had about 4-8 hours to deliver every step in my prototype. And I simply could not draw each of the 25 steps well enough, and digitize them, mainly because.... I'd need pictures of each step because I didn't know what each step looked like by heart anyway.

And then I realized: pictures! Of each step!

I'm not a photographer, but I do have an iPhone! And I could walk the route, and use those images for my prototype. Users would hopefully recognize they were "real" images, and I'd still be able to get feedback on what kind of imagery they expected (e.g. illustrations like Google Maps has, or this more "street-level" display)!

User Research

Over the course of a week in February 2026, I completed 9 in-person interviews.

There were 5 users that had at one point been frequent users (what I refer to as "Experts") of the Skyway, and 4 that were infrequent users ("Novices") of the Skyway. Note: users were also asked to rate their expertise on a scale of 1 through 5.

All participants brought their personal mobile device and I texted the prototype links to them after I introduced each task.

I documented my Research Notes in a Google Spreadsheet, including when I made prototype changes due to design/figma usability issues (that were no fault of my participants!) so that I could recall during analysis what functionality different users were seeing (or not seeing).

Figure 16. A screenshot of some of my research notes, including the prototype adjustments I made (highlighted in yellow.)

Research Findings

There is an audience for a Skyway navigation mobile app, even for Skyway “Experts,” who would feel empowered to add on Skyway tasks to their errand (e.g. grabbing lunch for the day AND dropping off dry-cleaning during 1 hour lunch break).

7/9 users successfully started their route.

Every participant had a strong, positive, verbal reaction once they started the route/navigation (with "real" photos of the route.)

No one abandoned the route even though it was more than 25 steps.

There was confusion about "where" users were on the map (at least 2 participants asked direct questions about the iconography on the map prior to starting the route.)

When tasked to start a route, users (especially “Experts”) found the category landing page cumbersome–“Experts” wanted to open the app directly to a map.

The "Events/Deals" did not resonate with participants (none of whom live Downtown).

For additional details and quotes, you can also check out my "one-page" report of my research findings.

Recommendations

Adjust the app so that:

  1. Users land directly on the map (and not a category page).
  2. Skip the page in route navigation where the user has entered their POI and sees it and their current location and must click "start route" again, so that users have fewer steps to start a route.
  3. Use visuals and iconography that align with other navigation apps/patterns.
  4. Show various POI based on how closely the user is zoomed.
  5. Reconsider how "Deals/Events" are presented/shared.
  6. Utilize category icons inside the pins to help indicate what "type" of POI are displaying.
  7. Consider renaming "Services" category, or breaking it into more specific categories.

Additional research opportunities:

  1. Naming the categories/grouping of the different Skyway POI and businesses.
  2. Community-building opportunities and presentation (with residents of Downtown Minneapolis, or perhaps people that work downtown more than 3 days a week.)

Mobile App Brand Materials:

Creating the App's Brand:

I wanted to keep the style of this app related to other Minnesota brands, but not the exact same (like when you draw on your eyebrows: they're "sisters", NOT "twins").

So I took great inspiration from State of Minnesota's Brand Guidelines for colors, and City of Minneapolis's voice guidelines for fonts and branding for the App's Style Guide (Lite).

Naming the App

I drafted a few name options and sketched potential logo ideas based off of the names and symbols that I associated with this app (things like: a compass, map "pin," directionality iconography, etc.) as well as imagerey for Minnesota (which is the "land of 10,000 lakes," and whose name derives from a Dakota phrase "Mni Sóta Makoce" which means the land where the water reflects the skies) and Minneapolis (the "City of lakes").

Figure 17. Sketches of different names and logo imagery for the app.

I chose to continue creating brand materials using the name "Skyway Navigator," although there is room for additional research testing app name options with potential users.

Typography

Because of Minneapolis's voice guidelines, and Minnesota's style guide, I knew I wanted to use a sans serif font (to be approachable and inclusive) but I wanted to incorporate a bit of contrast (while still remaining approachable) with a Serif font.

I used the newly identified name to see how fonts looked together, and displaying the words "Skyway Navigator" to determine the fonts for the app:

  1. Aglet Slab
  2. JAF Facit

Figure 18. Skyway Navigator Typography.

Colors

Again, I stuck with the overall trend of Minnesota brands using blue and green so that this app feels like it is a part of the Minnesota brand family, but I adjusted the colors from the Minnesota Style Guide to be a bit brighter (but still meeting WCAG 2.0 contrast requirements), and incorporated some gradients to bring a bit of joy and levity to the brand and experience.
