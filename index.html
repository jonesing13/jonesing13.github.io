Minneapolis Skyway Navigation Mobile App

Project Brief

In 2023, I moved back to Downtown Minneapolis in a flurry as my landlord had decided to sell the house I'd been renting for over a year. But as luck would have it, the first building I toured was connected to the Skyway–something the building manager used as a selling point for my apartment building; a little "nudge, nudge" to help me sign (today!). And... well, it didn't hurt.

The Minneapolis Skyway is a series of enclosed footbridges that connect buildings in Downtown Minneapolis to one another, helping pedestrians navigate the cold weather that comes around ever winter.

Most of these footbridges connect buildings on the second floor, but the entire system does also include some street-level and tunnel-level access points.

(“Minneapolis Skyway System”, Wikipedia, accessed January 28, 2026, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minneapolis_Skyway_System)

As I signed my lease and unpacked my belongings, I thought, "I'm going to use the Skyway! I'm going to rely less on my car, and it won't matter what time of year it is, because I'm on the Skyway! I'll be able to walk anywhere I need to downtown...."

But months passed and I avoided it.

In an effort to turn this ship around and learn about my neighborhood, I selected Walgreen's on Nicollet as my pharmacy. It's accessible on the Skyway–I could pick up any prescriptions during my lunch hour, since I worked from home, right?

And I did.

... Once or twice.

See, the thing about the Skyway is, it's intimidating: it doesn't conform to the neat and tidy grids of our streets; the signs are totally inconsistent (sometimes nonexistent!); and you might need to cross the same street twice (first going East, then going West) to get to the building you want to go to!

Perhaps most intimidating of all.... there are other people there using it! When you have to stop in every building to consult a picture of a map on your phone, impeding traffic (in what basically amounts to a hallway), full of other people (who are also on their lunch breaks)... It's easy to find reasons to just... not.

Project Hook

Personal reasoning and story/framing, including:

When I felt glued to my phone when I was trying to get from my apartment to the pharmacy. I wasn't sure how long it would take. My nerves about getting lost and taking even more time would often get the best of me, and I'd either drive there (and pay $15 to park in the lot), or get it delivered (for a slightly less expensive $10).

The Plan:

  1. Evaluate similar tools (like Google Maps, Apple Maps, Skywayaccess.com, Skyway.run)
  2. Draft low-fi mock-ups of a Skyway Mobile App
  3. Write a Research Plan, and get feedback from Experts (Brittany Schiesel)
  4. Build a prototype (or two) for user feedback
  5. Perform user interviews and usability tests
  6. Analyze user feedback and outline design recommendations for the Mobile App
  7. Create a Basic Style Guide for the platform, including colors, typography, and naming
  8. Identify future research opportunities and potential phases for deliverables

The Process:

  1. Evaluate existing tools
  2. Sketches/mock-ups of a Skyway Mobile App
  3. Research Planning, drafting a script, etc. (with Brittany Schiesel)
  4. Build a prototype (or two) for user feedback (struggled w navigation; illustrating all that? only showing 3-5 steps in the route? what verbiage is going to resonate?)
  5. Perform user interviews and usability tests
  6. Analyze user feedback and outline design recommendations for the Mobile App
  7. Create a Basic Style Guide for the platform, including colors, typography, and naming
  8. Identify future research opportunities and potential phases for deliverables (based off user feedback, needs for delivery, etc.)
