Minneapolis Skyway Navigation Mobile App

I've never gotten lost in the Mall of America...

In 2023, I moved back to Downtown Minneapolis in a flurry as my landlord had decided to sell the house I'd been renting for over a year. But as luck would have it, the first building I toured was connected to the Skyway–something the building manager used as a selling point for my apartment building; a little "nudge, nudge" to help me sign (today!). And... well, it didn't hurt.

Photo by Pete Sieger (“Minneapolis Skyways”, Behance, accessed March 26, 2026, https://www.behance.net/gallery/24796917/Minneapolis-Skyways)

The Minneapolis Skyway is a series of enclosed footbridges that connect buildings in Downtown Minneapolis to one another, helping pedestrians navigate the cold weather that comes around ever winter.

Most of these footbridges connect buildings on the second floor, but the entire system does also include some street-level and tunnel-level access points. (“Minneapolis Skyway System”, Wikipedia, accessed January 28, 2026, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minneapolis_Skyway_System)

As I signed my lease and unpacked my belongings, I thought, "I'm going to use the Skyway! I'm going to rely less on my car, and it won't matter what time of year it is, because I'm on the Skyway! I'll be able to walk anywhere I need to downtown...."

But months passed, and I found myself avoiding it.

The thing about the Skyway is: it's intimidating! It doesn't conform to the neat and tidy grids of our streets, the signs are totally inconsistent (sometimes nonexistent!), and you might need to cross the same street twice (first going East, then going West) to get to the building you want to go to! It's a total labyrinth.

Maybe most intimidating of all.... there are other people there using it! When you have to stop in every building to consult a picture of a map on your phone, impeding traffic (in what basically amounts to a hallway), full of other people (who seem much more familiar with the Skyway than you)... It's easy to find reasons to just... not use the Skwyay at all.

Research Analysis

Incorporate Britt's recommendations here:

When presenting findings, how you present really depends on who you are presenting to, what their goals are and how you want to influence them. For example if you were presenting and pitching to a potential partner to create this app, you might focus more on the concept development, interest, usefulness and features to include. If you are presenting to a team of developers to build it out, you may want to walk through the screens/prototypes and focus on usability, UI, flow.
  1. Make it short: use executive summaries and concise language so people will read and understand quickly. The executive summary is usually 1-2 slides of key takeaways (if someone read nothing else, this would be the basis of what they need to know)
  2. Show, don’t tell: use annotated mocks to show problem areas in the UI and/or a quick mock up/prototype to show recommendations. Short video clips supporting your evidence/findings will make them stronger
  3. Frame for your audience: frame the findings for your audience/ if it’s product/executive the messaging should be tailored toward how findings and recommendations will impact the business goals. If a more technical audience (designers/developers) insights and recommendations should be framed toward solutions and fixes and more detail oriented
